About the Hmongs

The Hmong people originated from northern China. However, we do not consider ourselves Chinese. Our languages are different, both spoken and written, and the cultural aspects are also different. Hmong people were considered a hill tribe because they lived mostly in the mountainous areas. There are still some Hmong people living in China today; their languages would probably have melded with the Chinese language a bit. The story that had been passed down was that the Chinese had tried to rule over the Hmong hill tribe, and when the Hmongs resisted, they were told to either leave the country or be executed. Many Hmong people fled China, mostly to Laos, Thailand, Vietnam, and the other neighboring countries. Those who chose to remain in China hid their identities and lived as Chinese citizens. It was just in the last decade that these Hmong-Chinese felt safe to come out of hiding and reclaimed their Hmong heritage. My great-grandparents fled and settled in Laos, and that was where my family lived until the Vietnam Secret War.


The Hmongs and The U.S. Government

Unbeknowest to a lot of Americans still, the Secret War was due to a stint the CIA conducted in the 1960s during the Vietnam War. The CIA dubbed it the Secret War and it was hidden from the American people for so long that this piece of American history is still not very well known today. Although the Secret War was finally revealed to the public in the 70s, they still left out how the Hmong people were detrimental to the "secret mission" in Laos. The Hmongs, simple hilltribe farmers in Laos, were turned into guerrilas soldiers to fight in a deadly war they couldn't win. The CIA promised the Hmongs sanctuary in the U.S. if they lose the war, but less than 4,000 Hmongs were allowed sanctuary in the U.S. Some were accepted into France and Austrailia, and the rest were left to fend for themselves against the communist rule in Laos. Many of the surviving soldiers and their families were labeled as rebels and were killed by the communists after the war. The soldiers who were able to escape prosecution, ran and hid in the jungles. Some are still hiding in the jungles of Laos even today.


In the mid-80's, my family and I were fortunate enough to escape to Thailand. My parents can still recall those times quite clearly: We traveled by foot, mostly at night, until we reached the Mekong River, which separated Thailand and Laos. One would need to have enough money to bribe a boat from Thailand to take his family across, otherwise, those who didn't have enough money or couldn't secure a boat, would have to swim across. Those people most often drowned. They also have to make sure they had enough money to bribe officials in Thailand if they were caught crossing. Anyone could be shot on the boat at any time by snipers on either side. Luckily, we were able to cross the river by boat and made it safely to Thailand.


Once in Thailand, Hmong people were put into refugee camps and were only able to be admitted into the U.S. if they had relatives among those 4,000 Hmongs who were first rescued, and even then, only if those relatives could support the refugees financially. My mom recalled the day we left the Thailand refugee camp called Ban Vinai. She said whenever buses came to take people bound for the U.S., the whole village would some to see them off, and it was the same that day. I think we can all agree the Hmongs have a very sad and tragic history, and because the U.S. government hid the details of the secret war for so long, Hmong people still do not get the recognition they deserved, for their sacrifices to the U.S. government.
